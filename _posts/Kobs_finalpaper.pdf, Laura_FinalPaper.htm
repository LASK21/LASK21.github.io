Introduction: Journey to the West (JTTW) is a 16th-century novel that was written and published by Wu Cheng’en during the Ming Dynasty in China, and it is widely considered to on be one of China’s greatest classical novels. The novel is four volumes describing the long pilgrimage of four Buddhist monks on their way to India in order to acquire Buddhist sacred scriptures from western Heaven and return them to Central China. The four monks consist of the Tang dynasty Sanzang, also known as Master Tripitaka, and his three disciples Sun Wukong, also known as the Monkey King, Zhu Bajie, and Sha Wujing, and their dragon turned horse companion Bai Long Ma. These five characters must endure trials and tribulations throughout the novel through their westward journey to India, facing obstacles in the forms of demons, weather, or corrupt humans in order to fulfill their duty and reach the west. 

JTTW heavily uses a combination of Chinese mythology, Buddhism, Confucianism, Taoism, and Chinese folklore for world-building and the symbolism found within the book, making it a very dense read and an overflowing narrative of events. Even with the heavy religious meanings and political allegories within the text the book is widely considered an adventure story that includes humor, satire, character growth, and offers a satisfying conclusion. It is because of these multiple elements that JTTW has been able to endure throughout 400 years of time and still be considered as widely popular, if not more so, in today’s day and age of media. It is important to recognize that the re-imaging of stories isn’t an abandonment of the original source material but rather a way to re-tell the story to a newer audience. Anyone can enjoy reading JTTW but when it comes to introducing over a hundred chapters to a new audience in a comprised, consistent manner there should be a certain method to do so practically. The media of television and movies is different from novelization as it is able to reach a wider audience than books or novels can on the basis that simply watching a story is able to reach young and old audiences and transcend the barriers of language.
